02Context & Problem

Why we needed to build this admin

What things looked like before we started

  • Course product maintenance lived in one admin, coupons in another, and promo activities in yet another. The capabilities were scattered, the interactions were inconsistent, and each system handled permissions in its own way.
  • Landing pages still relied on frontend engineers to write them by hand. Every new course campaign meant the usual full loop of request → scheduling → development → integration → release, and 3-5 days was the normal starting point.
  • Business rules like purchase limits and channel attribution were hardcoded on the backend, so even a simple adjustment meant a release.
  • The frontend projects for those older admins lived in different repos, with no shared component library, no shared request layer, and no shared permission model. Every new feature meant wiring up a fresh stack from scratch.

What problems that created

Low ops efficiency
Running one campaign meant working across 3-4 systems. The ops team used to joke that just switching accounts and systems already took half a day.
Frontend capacity got squeezed
Around 80% of the incoming work was still landing pages with very similar structure, so the team stayed stuck doing repetitive delivery work instead of platform work.
Poor data consistency
The same course data was maintained in multiple systems. If one side was updated and another was not, production issues were almost guaranteed.
Permissions were too coarse
Older back-office systems were basically “if you can log in, you can probably view and edit.” That made both auditing and compliance hard.

What the real need was

On the surface, the ask sounded like “build a new admin.” But the real problem was cross-team collaboration. We needed ops, product, frontend, and backend to work inside the same system, with the same data model and the same permission model. If we got that right, the frontend team could finally move from firefighting to building real platform capability.

What that meant for the frontend architecture

  • It had to scale. More business domains were definitely coming, so the architecture had to make it cheap to bring new modules in.
  • Permissions had to be fine-grained. Not just at the page level, but all the way down to buttons and operations.
  • Forms were going to be complex. Purchase-limit rules, channel configuration, and product setup all needed a reactive form solution that could handle deeply nested schemas.
  • Multi-environment control had to stay clean. Local, mock, gray, and production needed to stay separate and predictable.
  • Engineering discipline had to be there from day one. ESLint, Prettier, Stylelint, Husky, and lint-staged all needed to be in place early so multi-person collaboration would not turn messy.
