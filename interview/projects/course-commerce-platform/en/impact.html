Course Commerce Platform / Impact
08Impact

What practical changes came out of it

Some of these are hard numbers, and some are more qualitative but just as real. To be fair, some outcomes were team results rather than my solo contribution. My own role was mainly in the frontend architecture and the delivery of the key modules.

Quantifiable outcomes

3-5 days → hours
A new course landing page went from a 3-5 day frontend delivery cycle to something the ops team could finish in a few hours.
Business coverage
5 business domains · 20+ pages
Project Center, Gift Center, Tools, User Portrait, and Workspace.
Permission granularity
50+ permission keys
Covering both menus and buttons, and supporting fine-grained separation across 10+ roles.
Shared capability
~20 shared components · 10+ custom hooks
On average, each shared component was reused 10+ times.
List-page cost
500+ lines → ~150 lines
After the CRUD abstraction, a new list page shrank to about one third of the old size, with more consistent interaction.
Build environments
4 environments
dev / mock / gray / prod stayed separated, so testing and publishing could be done independently.
Frontend/backend contract
100% TypeScript coverage
All APIs were type-generated from Swagger, so backend interface changes showed up during frontend compile.
Landing-page templates
8 templates
Supporting regular, trial, half-screen, ad, lead-gen, combined, long-image, and media-training style scenarios.

Impact on team efficiency

Ops self-service rate
Landing-page work moved from “100% frontend-dependent” to “80%+ handled by ops,” with frontend mostly focusing on templates and components.
Frontend capacity released
Before the new admin, landing-page work could take more than 50% of the team’s weekly time. After launch, it dropped to under 10%, leaving room for actual platform work.
New teammate ramp-up
A new engineer could usually ship their first list page or form page within a week by following the shared patterns.
Cross-system switching cost
What used to require jumping across 3-4 back-office tools was pulled into one entry point.

Stability and quality

  • Since launch, there were no P0 frontend incidents. The few functional bugs we saw were fixed and shipped within hours.
  • Because the API contract was generated from Swagger, bugs caused by frontend/backend field mismatch dropped to nearly zero.
  • ESLint, Prettier, Stylelint, and Husky formed a solid engineering loop, so PR review could focus more on logic and less on formatting noise.
  • We ran permission-isolation checks across all 5 business domains, and there were no permission-leak incidents.

Impact on architecture and the wider org

Three things became reusable after this project
  • “Umi Max + two-layer access + axios interceptors + Swagger generation” became the default foundation for new internal admin projects in the team.
  • The CRUD primitive pattern — hooks like useTableSearch plus shared search and operation components — was pulled into an internal template that other projects could reuse.
  • Formily best practices for complex forms were documented, so later projects did not have to re-learn the same lessons from scratch.

What I personally got out of it

  • This was a full shift for me from “building pages” to “building the foundation.” Going from 0 to 1 on a fairly large internal admin made me feel very directly that “constraints are freedom” — clear conventions actually make business delivery faster.
  • I got much sharper about when abstraction is worth it and when it is not. Internal admin projects are especially easy to over-abstract, so restraint matters.
  • I also got much more real experience collaborating across frontend, backend, product, and ops. In B-side tools, the core question is not whether the code looks elegant. It is whether the tool genuinely helps the business team do their job.

Note: these numbers were observed over roughly 12 months after launch. Some outcomes were team-level results, not individual ones, and I tried to keep the scale honest rather than overstating it.
