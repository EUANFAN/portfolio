Low-Code Campaign Platform / Impact
08Impact

What actually changed after we shipped it

I try to describe the results in five buckets: delivery speed, business coverage, performance, stability, and team-level impact. That usually gives interviewers a much clearer picture than a generic “the platform worked well.”

Delivery speed

Campaign launch cycle
~2 days → ~15 minutes
The workflow moved from engineering delivery to self-serve publishing.
Minor page changes
Hours → minutes
Copy and image swaps stopped needing a full dev-test-release loop.
Frontend effort on campaigns
4-5 people → 0-1
Most frontend capacity moved from repetitive delivery into platform work.
Widget onboarding
~1 week → same day
Other teams could publish through the CLI and CDN contract instead of waiting on a platform release.

Business coverage

Business lines covered
4
The platform rolled out across the company’s main campaign-heavy lines.
Campaign pages served
1500+
That was the accumulated page count over the lifetime of the platform.
External widgets integrated
80+
This covered everything from course cards to countdowns and referral widgets.
Multi-channel delivery
30% → 90%+
A much larger share of campaigns started shipping through one schema to multiple channels.

Performance and UX

LCP
3.2s → 1.4s
SSR moved first paint into an acceptable range for traffic pages.
TTI
4.5s → 2.1s
Hydration was much cheaper than rebuilding the whole page on the client.
White-screen rate
2.3% → 0.15%
SSR plus graceful fallback materially improved reliability.
Client bundle size
~180KB stable
The three-tier widget strategy prevented the base bundle from growing with every new component.

Stability and quality

Publish success rate
99.9%+
SSR issues did not block release because the pipeline was designed with fallback.
Historic pages broken by upgrades
0 in 2 years
Version pinning in the schema kept old pages stable.
Visual mismatch bugs
3-5/week → <1/month
Shared rendering logic between editor and runtime paid off noticeably.
Runtime JS error rate
0.8% → 0.1%
The declarative trigger model narrowed the error surface.

Team and org impact

  • The frontend team changed shape. We went from campaign delivery work to owning reusable platform capability.
  • Ops became genuinely self-serve. Most campaigns no longer needed frontend scheduling to go live.
  • Other teams joined the widget ecosystem. That turned the platform into more of an internal marketplace than a closed tool.
  • The core schema and rendering model were reused elsewhere. Other internal page-building scenarios borrowed the same ideas.

What I personally got out of it

  • I got to drive a true platform project end to end, not just build a feature inside somebody else’s system.
  • I built stronger judgment around reactive state management, SSR in non-standard environments, and dynamic module systems.
  • I learned that platform work is only half technical. Adoption, training, and boundary-setting matter just as much.
  • I became much more deliberate about restraint: saying “we will not support that” is often what keeps a platform healthy.
